WHAT YOU NEED TO KNOW

KOTA DEPOK

History
Depok comes from the phrase "De Eerste Protestants Onderdaan Kerk" (DEPOK), which means the first Protestant church under Protestant rule. This city was formerly a colonial Dutch settlement.

Officially, Depok became an administrative city in 1982 and was later established as an autonomous city on April 27, 1999.

Depok is a city that combines the dynamics of modernization with traditional uniqueness. As a city of education and an economic center, Depok continues to grow without forgetting its cultural roots.

With various natural, cultural, and religious tourist attractions, Depok is a destination worth visiting or making a place to live.

Geography and Culture
Depok City is a city in West Java province, Indonesia, located between Jakarta to the north, Bogor to the east and south, and South Tangerang to the west.

As one of the supporting cities for the capital city of Jakarta, Depok has rapidly developed into a center of urbanization, education, and business, without losing its traditional and cultural appeal.

Depok is one of the cities with fast population growth. Its residents consist of various ethnic groups, with the Betawi community as the native population, along with migrants from various regions.

Transportation and Green Spaces
The growth of residential and business areas makes Depok a strategic location for Jakarta residents seeking housing.

Depok has good transportation access, including the KRL Commuter Line that connects Depok with Jakarta and Bogor, as well as toll roads, such as the Cinere-Jagorawi Toll Road (Cijago), which facilitates private vehicle access.

Additionally, Depok still has many green spaces, such as the Urban Forest of Universitas Indonesia and several natural lakes (setu), like Situ Pengasinan, Situ Cilodong, and Situ Sawangan.

Challenges of Depok City

  1. Traffic congestion: As a supporting city of Jakarta, Depok faces congestion issues, especially on main roads like Margonda Raya.
  2. Urbanization: Rapid population growth puts pressure on infrastructure and public facilities.
  3. Culture: In the face of modernization, preserving local cultural heritage, such as Betawi traditions, presents a unique challenge.
